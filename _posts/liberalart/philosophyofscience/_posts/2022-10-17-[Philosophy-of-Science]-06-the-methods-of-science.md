## 1. Observation and Experiment

### 1.1 Features of Observation and Experiments

- Scientists make observations and perform experiments to justify their hypotheses
- **Observation** : A passive affair in that observers do not do anything to a target object
- **Experiment** : An active affair in that experimenters do something to a target object
- No scientist attempts to establish a hypothesis without making observations or performing experiments, i.e., without presenting empirical evidence

### 1.2 Examples

- **Alfred Wegener** (1880-1930) observed the world map, noticed that the western coastline of Africa fits the eastern coastline of South American
  - Then, he hypothesized that the two continents were once conjoined and then gradually drifted apart from each other
  - If he had not observed the world map, he would not have hit upon the continental drift theory
- Scientists observe the world, discover a regularity, and then make a prediction about a future event
  - Scientists observe that Halley's Comet appeared in 1935, 1910, and 1986
    - They predict that it will appear again in 2061
- Finding a regularity might lead to a discovery of a law
  - **Tycho Brahe** (1546-1601), the greatest naked-eye observer in the history of science, collected extensive data about the position of planets in the night sky
  - **Johannes Kepler** (1571-1630) borrowed Brahe's data and discovered the three laws of planetary motion
- Scientists perform experiments to test their hypotheses
  - Many women were dying while delivering babies in the first maternity division of the Viena General Hospital in 1846
  - Medical students performed autopsy in an autopsy room, and then assisted the women delivering babies
  - **Ignaz Semmeiweis** (1818-1865) hypothesized that cadaverous materials on medical students' hands were killing the women in the first maternity division
    - He had medical students wash hands before they assisted the mothers
    - As a result, the mortality rate dropped dramatically

### 1.3 Instruments for Experiments

- Scientists use instruments, such as telescopes, microscopes, and particle accelerators, to make better observations and to perform better experiments
- Scientific instruments enable scientists to observe what they cannot observe with their naked eyes and to perform experiments that they cannot with their bare hands

## 2. Thought Experiment

### 2.1 Definition

<u>Thought experiment</u> : An experiment performed in imagination

### 2.2 Example

**Einstein** (1879-1955) performs a thought experiment to show that acceleration and mass have the same effect of bending light

## 3. Idealized Models

### 3.1 Definition

<u>Idealized model</u> : Purposefully simplified and distorted target system

### 3.2 Example

- **Kinetic model** : Similar to real gas in some respects, but dissimilar to the real gas in other respects.
- **Subway map** : It ignores depths of stations and distorts the distance between stations. But it is more convenient to use the simplified subway map than an accurate map.

### 3.3 Advantage of Idealized Models

- An idealized model is not an exact copy of a target system
  - It does not reflect the target system
- Therefore, it is false
- Although false, it is useful to make true predictions about observables
  - Antirealists would say science does not aim to give us theoretical truth

## 4. Infernce to the Best Explanation

### 4.1 Definition

<u>Inference to the Best Explanation</u>(IBE) : Scientists make an inference from phenomena to the hypothesis that best explains them

### 4.2 Example

- IBE is the main rule of inference that **Charles Darwin** uses to establish evolutionary theory
  - Darwin infers evolutionary theory on the grounds that it best explains analogies
- The inference from observational evidence to a hypothesis is inductive, i.e., the evidence makes it likely that the hypothesis is true
  - The evidence does not *guarantee* that the hypothesis is true

## 5. Inference from a hypothesis to observational evidence

Suppose that there were dinosaurs, and that they were properly buried and preserved. If such conditions are met, the inference is deductive

- There were dinosaurs. (Hypothesis)
- Some of them were properly buried and preserved. (Initial Condition)
- $\therefore$ Fossils of some dinosaurs exist. (Observational Consequences)

## 6. Methods to Conceive of Hypotheses

### 6.1 Various Methods to Conceive of Hypotheses

- Method of swimming

  Swimming improves one's blood circulation. As a result, one's brain functions better and produce new ides
- Method of intellectual discussion

  **Louis de Broglie** (1892-1987) had an intellectual conversation with other physicists over the nature of microscopic objects, and it occurred to him that an electron is a wave just like a photon.
- Method of daydreaming

  **August Kekule** (1829-1896) allegedly had a daydream in front of a fire place. In the daydream, a snake bit its tail. Inspired by the daydream, he proposed the hypothesis that benzene has a hexagonal struture

### 6.2 Importance of Experiment

- Method of daydreaming is not a reliable method
- However, we can accept a hypothesis produced by the method of daydreaming *insofaras* we justify it by performing experiments

## 7. Summary

- Observation is a passive affair in that observers do not do anything to a target object
- Experiment is an active affair in that experimenters do something to a target object
- A real experiment is performed in the real world, while a thought experiment is an experiment performed in imagination
- An idealized model simplifies a target system, and thus it is false
- IBE is a rule of inference that scientists use when they infer the hypothesis that besyt explains phenomena
- Even if scientists used the method of daydreaming to conceive of a hypothesis, we can trust the hypothesis insofaras it passed an empirical test

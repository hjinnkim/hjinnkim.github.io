## 1. Confirmation

### 1.1 Definition

*<u>Confirmationism</u>* holds that a hypothesis can be confirmed by obsrvations, i.e., that observational evidence can show that a hypothesis is likely to be true

### 1.2 How Scientists Confirm a Hypothesis

1. They imagine what observable event must occur, if a hypothesis were true
2. They check whether the observable event occurs or not
    - If the observable event occurs, the hypothesis is confirmed
    - If the observable event does not occur, the hypothesis is disconfirmed

In other words

1. Scientists set up a hypothesis
2. Deduce an observational consequence from it along with initial conditions
3. Compare the observational consequence with the world
    - If the observational consequence agrees with the world, the hypothesis is confirmed
    - If the observational consequence disagrees with the world, the hypothesis is disconfirmed

In sum,
- A true observational consequence makes a hypothesis likely to be true
- A false observational consequence makes a hypothesis likely to be false

This method of confirming a hypothesis is called *<u>the hypothetico-deductive method of confirmation</u>*

### 1.3 Problems with Confirmationism

The existence of rival hypotheses makes it questionable whether observational evidence makes a hypothesis likely to be true
- The original hypothesis and its rival hypotheses make different claims about unobservables, but they more or less make the same claims about observables
- This problem is called *the problem of underdetermination*
- The idea is that observations do not determine which of the rival hypotheses is true

## 2. Falsificationism

### 2.1 Definition

*<u>Falsificationism</u>* (**Karl Popper**, 1963) holds that a scientific theory is falsifiable, i.e., it is "capable of conflicting with possible, or conceivable, observations" (Popper, 1963)

### 2.2 Logical Relationship between Observation and Hypothesis

- Popper adimits that observations cannot prove a hypothesis to be ture
- But he argues that they can prove it to be false, i.e., that they can falsify it

### 2.3 Discontent with Superposition and Pseudoscience

- A theory that allows both an event and its opposite event is not falsifiable, hence not scientific
- The belief in the god and Adler's theory are immune to refutation even by a conceivable observation
- Therefore, they are not falsifiable

### 2.4 The Aim of Science

- According to falsificationism, scientific develoipment consists of a series of conjectures and refutations
- An aim of science is not to verify hypotheses but rather to falsify them
- Hypotheses become more and more falsifiable as science progresses because their scopes become broader

### 2.5 Problems with Falsificationism

- It clasheds with the historical episodes in which scientist retained theories and discarded observational data when theories conflicted with observationakl data
- Falsificationism cannot accommodate the historical episodes in which scientists dogmatically hang onto their pet theories and threw out *auxiliary assumptions* instead of throwing out their pet theories

## 3. Holism

### 3.1 Definition

*<u>Holism</u>* holds that it is not a single theory but rather a group of a theory and auxiliary assumptions that is tested against observation

An auxiliary assumption is an assumption that is necessary for a theory to be tested against observation

### 3.2 Features of Holism

- When the set clashes with observation, scientists do not need to give up a theory
- They may instead give up an auxiliary assumption 
  - In other words, observation does not necessarily discredit a theory
  - It may rather discredit an auxiliary assumption
- In general, negating an auxiliary assumption allows scientists to hang onto their pet theory without committing a contradiction when it clashes with an experiment result

### 4. Summary

- Confirmation holds that a hypothesis can be confirmed by observations
- According to the hypothetico-deductive method of confirmation, we deduce an observational consequence from a set of a hypothesis and initial conditions, and then compare the observational consequence with the world
  - If it agrees with the world, the main hypothesis is confirmed
  - If it disagrees with the world, the main hypothesis is disconfirmed
  - The problems of underdetermination, however, undermines confirmationism
- Falsificationism asserts that a scientific theory is falsifiable
  - The general theory of relativity is falsifiable because we can conceive of observational events that would refute it
  - By contrast, the belief in the god and Adler's theory are not falsifiable because we cannot conceive of observational events that would rebut them
  - Scientists reject a scientific theory when it conflicts with observations
  - Scientific development consists in a series of conjectures and refutations
  - Objectors to falsificationism point out that scientists sometimes rejected not their pet theories but rather their observational beliefs and auxiliary assumptions in the history of science
- Holism claims that it is not a single hypothesis but rather a group of hypotheses that meets the tribunal of experince, so when a group of hypotheses conflicts with observation, scientists may condemn an auxiliary hypothesis rather than the main hypothesis

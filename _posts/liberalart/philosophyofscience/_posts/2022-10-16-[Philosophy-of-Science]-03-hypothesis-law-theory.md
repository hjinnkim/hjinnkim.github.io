## 1. Hypothesis

### 1.1 Definition

1. There are ten dimensions of space and one dimension of time
2. A large asteroid hit the Yucatan Peninsula about 65 million years ago
3. $F=Fm_1m_2/r^2$
4. The total amount of energy is conserved in a closed system

These four examples of hypotheses provide a clue about *what a hypothesis is*.

- *<u>Hypotheis</u>* is an assumption proposed to explain or to predict some events

### 1.2 Confirmation of Hypotheses

- Some hypotheses are *confirmed* while other hypotheses are *not*
- i.e., there is good observational evidence for some hypotheses, but not for other hypotheses
- Confirmed hypotheses are likely to be true, while other is not

## 2. Law

### 2.1 Three Distinctive Features

A law has at least the following three distinctive features.

1. A law is a *general* statement

    - A general statement is a description of a group of events or a group of objects

        (ex) $F=Gm_1m_2/r^2$)
    - A particular statement is a statement about a particular event or a particular object
    
        (ex) a large asteroid hit the Yucatan Peninsula about 65 million years ago)

2. A law is *comfirmed*

    - If there is not good evidence for a hypothesis, it cannot be a law, although it is a general statement
3. A law is a necessary statement (Hung, 1997)

    - A law asserts why an event must occur

        (ex) the law of gravity asserts that two material objects must attract each other)

    - "All coins in my pocket are silver". This statement is a general statement, but it is not necessary statement. Instead, it is a *contingent* statement. It is just an accident that all the coins in my pocket are silver. This kind of statement is called an "accidental generalization"

### 2.2 Truth of a Law

- A law does not have to be true, i.e., it does not have to be justified.

  - Consider the law of gravity. It asserts that the sun exerts a gravitational force on the earth instantly, although it is far from the earth. *Action at a distance*, however, is impossible according to the special theory of relativity, which states that nothing travels faster than light. Moreover, the general theory of relativity explains the motion of the earth not in terms of the gravitational force between the earth and the sun but in terms of the curvature of the spacetime created by the sun. It follows, then, that we have a good reason to believe that the law of gravity is false.
  - Nevertheless, $F=Gm_1m_2/r^2$ is still called *<u>the law of gravity</u>*.
- An alternative view is that the law of gravity is not a law, although we still call it "the law of gravity"
  
  - It is at best a *law-like* statement, i.e., it is a statement that looks like a law, but  it is not a law.
  - An interesting challenge to this position is whether we should say the same thing about other laws, such as the first law of thermodynamics and the Boyle-Charles law.
  - We cannot know whether putative(추정되는) laws are genuine laws or law-like statements

## 3 Theory

### 3.1 Definition

**Hempel's view** (1966) about the structure of scientific theories.

  - Scientific theory : A collection of statements, some of which are *internal principles*, and others of which are *bridge principles*.
  - A *internal principle* describes how theoretical entities behave. Thus, it is couched in purely theoretical terms.
  - A *bridge principle* depicts how the behavior of the theoretical entities is related to that of observational entities.
  - Therefore, a scientific theory performs the function of reflecting unobservables.

### 3.2 The Five Functions

1. A scientific theory *explains* phenomena
2. A scientific theory *predicts* future events
3. A scientific theory helps us to *manipulate* things in the world
4. A scientific theory *unifies* diverse phenomena
5. A scientific theory *reflects* the world

    -  An interesing issue is whether a scientific theory correctly or incorrectly describes the world

## 4. Similarities and Differencies

### 4.1 Hypothesis vs. Law

- Similarities
  - They explain and predict phenomena
  - They are expressed in one sentence
- Differencies
  - A law is confirmed **vs.** A hypothesis might not be
  - A hypothesis can take either the form of a particular statement or the form of a general statement **vs.** A law can only take the form of a general statement

### 4.2 Theory

- Similarities
  - A theory explains and predicts phenomena
- Differences
  - A theory is not expressed by one statement but rather by a set of statements
  - Hypotheses and laws can serve as contituents of a theory
    - In this reason, a school of philosophy of sciencer called "logical positivism" defines a theory as a set of hypotheses at least one of which is a law

### 4.3 Change of Hypothesis in Theory

When science progresses, an old hypothesis is often taken out of a theory and a new hypothesis is added to it

- Darwin's hypothesis is replaced by Eldredge and Gould's hypothesis in evolutionary theory
- Thus, contemporary evolutionary theory is slightly different from Darwin's evolutionary theory

### 4.4 Misunderstanding about Theory

Some people claim that a law is proven, whereas a theory is not
- i.e., there is sufficient evidence for a law, but not for a theory
- Thus, on this view, a theory would become a law once it acquired enough evidence for it
- Consider, however, that the special theory of relativity includes the law, $E=mc^2$, and that a law is confirmed
- Given that a law can be a component of a theory, it is wrong to say that a law is proven while a theory is not
- A theory can be as highly proven as a law

### 4.5 Caveat about these differences

The distinction among them is not a strict one. Indeed, scientists do no fuss about the differences among them, using the three concepts, to some extent, interchangeably. For example, they sometimes call the law of gravity "the *theory* of gravity."

## 5. Summary

- A hypothesis is a statement that is proposed to explain or predict events
- A law is also a statement that is proposed to explain or predict events
  - Only a general statement, however, can be a law of nature
  - It is confirmed
  - It is a necessary statement
  - It is controversial whether it must be true
- According to Hempel, a theory is a set of statements, some of which are internal principles and others of which are bridge principles
  - It performs the five functions: explanation, prediction, manipulation, unification, and reflection

## 2. Truth

### 2.1 Correspondence Theory

**Aristotle** (384-322 B.C.) advanced **<u>Correspondence Theory of truth</u>**

- **Correspondence Theory** : A statement is true if and only if it corresponds to the world, and it is false if and only if it does not corresponds to the world

### 2.2 Other Truth-bearers

- Beliefs
- Opinions
- Claims
- Views
- Hypotheses
- Laws
- Theories

## 3. Argument

### 3.1 Definition

A set of statements in which some of them are *<u>premises</u>* and one of them is the *<u>conclusion</u>*

### 3.2 Example

- Some apples are red
- $\therefore$ All appples are red

The first statement is a *premise* and the seconde statement is a *conclusion*.

### 3.3 Hypothesis

Scientists construct and refute arguments. Scientists do not merely state their *<u>hypotheses</u>*.They present *evidence* to persuade their readers that their *hypotheses* are true. The *evidence* is the premise, and the *hypothesis* is the conclusion.

## 4. Deductive vs. Inductive

### 4.1 Definition

- **Deductive Argument** : If premises are true, the conclusion is guaranteed to be true
- **Inductive Argument** : Even if premises are true, it is likely, but less than 100% probable, that the conclusion is.

### 4.2 Examples

- Deductive Argument
  
  100% of UNIST students are intelligent

  Alice is a UNIST student

  $\therefore$ She is intelligent

- Inductive Argument

  90% of UNIST students are intelligent

  Alice is a UNIST student

  $\therefore$ She is intelligent

### 4.3 Easy Way to Distinguish

  Investigate whether the given argument has a small *<u>possibility</u>* that the *<u>conclusion</u>* could be *<u>false</u>* when premises are true

## 5. Observational vs. Theoretical

### 5.1 Definition

- **Observational entity** : We can observe them with our naked eyes
- **Theoretical entity** : We cannot observe them with our naked eyes

### 5.2 Examples

- Observational entity

  stones, trees, chairs, and computers
- Theoretical entity

    electrons, quarks, black holes, dark energy, dark matter, and neutrinos

### 5.3 Observational/Theoretical Terms

An *<u>observational term</u>* refers to an *<u>observational entity</u>* and a *<u>theoretical term</u>* refers to a *<u>theoretical entity</u>*.

Only *<u>observational terms</u>* figure in an observational statement, whereas a *<u>theoretical term</u>* features in a *<u>theoretical statement</u>*.

### 5.4 Importance of Distinction between Observational/Theoretical Entitiy

- An *observational statement* can be determined whether it is true or false just by observing a relevant part of the world.
- An *theoretical statement* cannot be determined whether it is true or false just by observing a part of the world.
- Relatedly, our evidence for an observational statement is more powerful than our evidence for a theoretical statement.
- A perennial topic in philosophy of science is whether or not we have a sufficient evidence for believing that a theoretical statement is true.

### 5.5 Classic Objection to the Distinction between Observational/Theoretical Entities

Maxwell said we cannot draw a line between them

- We can observe a tree through a windowpane $\rightarrow$ the tree is an observational entity.
- We can observe a bird in a distant tree through binoculars $\rightarrow$ the bird is an observational entity.
- We seem to observe Jupiter with a telescope $\rightarrow$ Jupiter seems to be an observational entity.

How about:

- Cell that we seem to observe with a light microscope
- DNA that we seem to observe with an electron microscope

It appears that *there is no strict distinction between observational and theoretical entities*.

### 5.6 Refute to the Previous Objection

*Bas van Fraassen* retorts that the foregoing objection only shows that the two predicates, "observational" and "theoretical" are vague. A predicate is "usable provided it has clear cases and clear counter-cases"

- We cannot draw a line between boys and men $\leftarrow$ However, some people are clearly boys, and other people are clearly men. 
- Similarly, there are clear cases and counter-cases of "observational" and "theoretical" $\leftarrow$ A tree is clearly an observational entity, and an electron is clearly a theoretical entity.

## 6. Summary

- True statements correspond to the world, whereas false statements do not.
- An argument is a set of statements, some of which are premises and one of which is the conclusion.
- In a deductive argument, the conclusion necessarily follows from premises.
- In an inductive argument, the conclusion probably follows from premises.
- An observational entity is an entity that we can observe, whereas a theoretical entity is an entity that we cannot observe.
- It is more difficult to determine whether a theoretical statement is true or false than to determine whether an observational statement is true or false.
